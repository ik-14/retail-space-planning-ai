## Problem Statement

Mid-market retailers ($100M-$2B revenue, 50-500 stores) spend enormous time creating planograms manually in legacy tools (AS400, Spaceman, Excel). Space planners are under constant deadline pressure from management to publish planograms before range changes hit. When a range change arrives, the entire process restarts. Nobody verifies whether stores actually implement the planograms (industry compliance is 40-60%), and nobody measures which layouts drive revenue.

The founder witnessed this firsthand over 3 years at Holland & Barrett, building the internal planogramming platform that was meant to replace legacy tooling.

## Demand Evidence

- Holland & Barrett funded an entire engineering team to escape legacy space planning tools (Spaceman, AS400). That's a real budget allocation — hundreds of thousands in engineering salary spent on this problem.
- The internal platform the founder built had real adoption from the space planning team. People used it daily.
- Even after building the new platform, space planners still spent hours doing repetitive drag-and-drop work. The tool made creation possible but didn't eliminate the manual grind.
- Managers were "on their backs" about deadlines. Range changes meant starting over. The pressure was recurring and structural, not one-off.
- H&B had no analytics or compliance verification. They sent people physically to check stores or relied on sales data after the fact.

**Gap in evidence:** All demand signal comes from one retailer (Holland & Barrett). No conversations with other mid-market retailers to confirm the pain exists identically elsewhere. The founder explicitly stated: "I don't know what other retailers are using."

## Status Quo

At Holland & Barrett (the one validated data point):
1. Legacy tools: AS400 and Spaceman for planogram creation
2. Excel for SKU lists and supporting data
3. Manual drag-and-drop planogram building in the new internal platform
4. Hours per planogram, deadline pressure from management
5. Range changes = complete restart
6. No compliance checking (sent people physically or checked sales data post-hoc)
7. No measurement of which layouts actually drove revenue

For other mid-market retailers: unknown. Research suggests ~40% still use Excel/manual methods, but this is unvalidated.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Target user:** The head of space planning at a UK mid-market retailer (50-300 stores). Budget owner. Cares about: speed of planogram delivery, revenue per square foot, team productivity.

**Secondary user:** Space planners themselves — the people doing 4-8 hours of manual work per planogram under deadline pressure.

**Narrowest wedge (NOT the product yet):** A conversation. "Give me 30 minutes. I'll show you what I saw at Holland & Barrett and ask what your world looks like. If the pain matches, I'll build a demo for your specific workflow — free."

## Constraints

- Solo founder (technical, 3 years retail domain experience)
- No funding yet
- No validated demand outside H&B
- Competitive landscape is more crowded than initially researched (LEAFIO: 200+ customers, Shelfr, Space.AI, Planomind, Nexgen POG all building in this space)
- Enterprise sales cycles may be long even for mid-market
- Access to retailer data (planograms + POS) required for any meaningful demo

## Premises

1. At H&B, space planners spent hours on repetitive planogram creation under tight deadlines. This is validated.
2. The sharpest pain observed is speed of creation under deadline pressure — not compliance, not analytics. (Founder's best guess, not externally validated.)
3. The founder can build a first version solo using modern AI tooling.
4. Whether other retailers have the same pain in the same way is UNKNOWN — this is the critical assumption to validate.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: "Validation Sprint" — Customer Discovery First

Don't build product yet. Spend 4-6 weeks talking to 15-20 mid-market retailers (ex-colleagues, LinkedIn, cold outreach). Validate that the pain observed at H&B exists elsewhere and learn what competitors they currently use. Then build based on what you hear.

- Effort: S (4-6 weeks, no code)
- Risk: Low
- Pros: Eliminates the #1 gap ("I don't know what others use"), conversations can become LOIs, might discover the real pain is different than assumed
- Cons: Feels like not building, competitors are shipping now, might learn market is saturated

### Approach B: "Wedge Demo" — Build Narrow Diagnostic While Talking

Build a narrow tool in 3 weeks (upload planogram image + POS data, get back insights on what's broken). Use it to get meetings. Talk to people in parallel.

- Effort: M (3-4 weeks build + outreach)
- Risk: Medium
- Pros: Something tangible to show, validates product and market simultaneously
- Cons: Might build the wrong wedge, requires sample data, market already has funded competitors

### Approach C: "Compliance-First" — Own Mobile Shelf Verification

Skip planogram generation (crowded). Own the verification layer: "Take a photo of your shelf, we tell you what's wrong." Gap between Trax (expensive, enterprise) and nothing.

- Effort: M (4-6 weeks for CV prototype)
- Risk: Medium-High
- Pros: Less competitive angle, instant value without integration, natural expansion path
- Cons: Harder technical challenge, smaller initial deal size, not deepest domain expertise

## Recommended Approach

**Approach A: Validation Sprint.** The founder chose this because the landscape research revealed the market is more crowded than initially assumed (6+ funded competitors in AI planogramming). Building without talking to customers risks becoming competitor #7 in a space where LEAFIO already has 200+ customers. The validation sprint costs nothing except time and eliminates the biggest risk: that the H&B experience is an outlier, not a pattern.

## Open Questions

1. Do other UK mid-market retailers (Superdrug, Co-op, WHSmith, Boots) experience the same pain as H&B, or do they have different workflows/tools?
2. Are retailers aware of LEAFIO, Shelfr, Space.AI, and other new entrants? If so, why haven't they bought?
3. Is planogram GENERATION the real pain, or is it compliance/verification/analytics?
4. Would retailers talk to a solo founder, or do they only engage with established vendors?
5. Is the mid-market buying cycle actually faster than enterprise (claimed 2-4 months vs 12+)?
6. Can you get access to real planogram + POS data from willing retailers for a demo?

## Success Criteria

Target: 15-20 conversations with space planning decision-makers at mid-market retailers.

**Tiered outcome framework:**
- **15-20 conversations** = strong signal. Make build/pivot/pass decision with confidence.
- **8-14 conversations** = moderate signal. Extend sprint by 2 weeks, shift to events (Retail Week, NRF), cold email via LinkedIn Sales Nav, or ask existing contacts for second-degree intros.
- **Fewer than 8 in 6 weeks** = access itself is a barrier. Reassess whether mid-market space planning is reachable by a solo founder without brand or warm network. Consider: partnering with someone who has the rolodex, or pivoting to a different entry angle (store-level staff rather than HQ planners).

**Per-conversation success:**
- Clear answer to: "What tools do you use today, and what's broken about them?"
- 3+ retailers who express willingness to pilot (minimum: "Yes, send me a demo when you have one" on a recorded call; ideal: signed LOI or paid pilot commitment)
- Validated understanding of whether generation, compliance, or analytics is the sharpest pain

**End-of-sprint decision:** build (and what), pivot (different angle), or pass (market too crowded/pain too weak)
